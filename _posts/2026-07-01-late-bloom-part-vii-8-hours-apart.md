*"Eight p.m. for you is four a.m. for me"*

This is Part VII of **[Late Bloom Stories](/series/late-bloom-stories/)** — 300 episodes of romance with happy endings, loneliness, random chat, and courage to reach out again. After [Part VI](/late-bloom-part-vi-noodle-shop-number-seven/).
## Seattle

This is the story of Kevin and Chloe.

## When life got in the way

Kevin worked as a barista. Chloe was a travel blogger.

They met near the lakefront in Seattle — not like a movie. Two tired adults whose worlds shrank until they sat close enough to talk.

Evenings became walks. Morning texts. *Are you home yet?* Love without fireworks — the kind that grows because both people had already learned what losing feels like.

Then life broke in. Long distance — one studying abroad, one staying behind.

Not because love ended. Because life is heavier than feeling sometimes — family, money, pride, timing, fear.

Kevin let go first — thinking he was protecting Chloe. He said the hardest sentence he could find. She cried, or went quiet. They parted without a ritual goodbye. Only space.

On the loneliest nights, Kevin opened his browser — a random text chat, no app required. Not to find someone new. Not to replace Chloe. Just to hear a stranger say *hey* — proof that another human was awake in Seattle.

## Lonely nights, random chat

Chloe did the same. She tried voice chat after midnight — voice chat at midnight was the only hour their time zones overlapped. They never met on that screen. Never traded real names. A few sentences, then close the tab — loneliness still there, but a little lighter.

They did not know the person they would meet offline was sometimes the one they needed all along. Random chat does not create love. It keeps the heart from freezing completely — so when love returns, there is still room for it.

Time passed. Kevin rebuilt a modest life. Chloe did too. They did not delete numbers. They did not call. They did not forget.

One day at the lakefront, chance — or something like it — put them in the same frame again.

A one-way ticket — no warning text, just arrival.

Someone said: *Eight p.m. for you is four a.m. for me* — the line only former lovers use when pretending to be strangers.

## The ending you hoped for

They sat down. Told the truth. Random chat had softened lonely nights — but only presence could finish the story.

Same time zone, same breakfast table. A year or two later they chose each other again — not from loneliness. From love.
